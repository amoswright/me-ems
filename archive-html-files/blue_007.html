Respiratory Distress with Bronchospasm #2

(COPD, Emphysema, Chronic Bronchitis, Asthma)

  1. Dexamethasone

    1. Adult:
      1. 16 mg PO x 1 if patient can tolerate oral medications, is not in overt respiratory distress, and is not requiring an airway management technique that obscures access to the mouth (e.g., O₂ mask or CPAP),
      2. 16 mg IV/IM/IO x 1, if the patient cannot tolerate oral medications, or has any of the above conditions.
    2. Pediatric:
      1. 0.6 mg/kg (single MAX dose of 16 mg) PO x 1, if patient can tolerate oral medications, is not in overt respiratory distress, and is not requiring an airway management technique that obscures access to the mouth (e.g., O₂ mask or CPAP), <img>Bear</img>
      2. 0.6 mg/kg (single MAX dose of 16 mg) IV/IM/IO x 1, if the patient cannot tolerate oral medications, or has any of the above conditions.

  2. For patients not improving with the above measures, consider the following OPTIONS:

    1. Repeated or continuous albuterol by nebulization or inhaler.
    2. For patients in status asthmaticus:
      1. EPINEPHrine
        1. Adult: EPINEPHrine 0.3 mg IM of 1mg/1mL every 20 minutes. <img>Hospital</img>
        2. Pediatric: EPINEPHrine dose which is as follows:
    3. Less than 25 kg, 0.15 mg IM [0.15mL of 1mg/mL] in anterolateral thigh every 20 minutes,
    4. Greater than 25 kg, 0.3 mg IM [0.3 mL of 1mg/mL] in anterolateral thigh every 20 minutes. <img>Bear</img>
      1. Magnesium Sulfate
        1. Adult: Magnesium Sulfate 2 grams IV/IO, diluted in 50-100 mL of IVF (D₅W preferred), over 15 minutes, delivered via medication pump.
        2. Pediatric: Magnesium Sulfate 50 mg/kg IV/IO with a MAX dose of 2 grams, diluted in 50-100 mL of IVF (D₅W preferred), over 15 minutes, delivered via medication pump. <img>Bear</img>

Asthmatic patients:
Airway management of asthmatic patients is primarily pharmacological, not mechanical. Therefore, the focus should be on taking those actions that enable the clinician to provide inhaled bronchodilatorsators and, in patients with severe bronchospasm, obtain rapid IV or IO access, administer IV dexamethasone, IV magnesium, and consider IM EPINEPHrine. Due to the pathophysiology of asthma, positive pressure ventilation (facemask, BIAD, or endotracheal intubation) rarely, if ever, is an effective treatment without pharmacological intervention. Therefore, unless the patient is apneic, provide supplemental oxygen via non-rebreather and focus on providing pharmacological interventions. CPAP, with the lowest PEEP setting possible, is a last resort and a bridge to intubation. Specifically, if CPAP is going to be used in the asthmatic, continuous nebs must be administered simultaneously and the clinician must be prepared to proceed with advanced airway management.
