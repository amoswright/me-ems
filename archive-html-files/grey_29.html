Care of the Deaf and Hard of Hearing #1

Excellent communication with patients is essential for EMS clinicians to meet their goals of patient care. Interpreter services are essential resources for communication with patients who do not speak the clinician's primary language. Visual interpreter services may not be immediately available for communication with deaf and hard of hearing, therefore refer to the following guidelines.

<img>Hospital Logo</img>

EMT/Advanced EMT/Paramedic

  1. Elicit information from the patient and family regarding preferred method of communication (handwritten, typing, speech (lip) read, American Sign Language (ASL), Assessment Tool).
  2. Refer to the EMS Assessment Tool for the Deaf and Hard of Hearing, Grey 30.
  3. Utilize handwritten or digital communication (e.g., tablet), if available.
  4. Bring any devices (e.g., hearing aids, cochlear implants) to the hospital.
  5. Notify hospital of incoming patient and need for ASL interpreter, if needed.
  6. If the patient consents, and if available, bring an adult family member capable of interpreting ASL. Certified translators are the standard of care. In emergent situations, adult family members may be used to assist with translation until a certified translator is available.
