Adult Cardiac Arrest #2

  1. Consider causes of OHCA. Consult OLMC early for questions/advice.

    1. Is patient on dialysis, have known renal disease or suspicion for hyperkalemia?
      1. Calcium gluconate 3 gram bolus IV/IO, diluted in a minimum of 10 mL of IVF, over 1 minute
      2. Sodium bicarbonate 50 mEq bolus IV/IO; may repeat x 2
      3. IV fluid bolus
    2. Is patient suspected TCA or other suspected sodium channel blocker overdose? (See Pearls)
      1. Sodium bicarbonate 50 mEq bolus IV/IO; may repeat x 2
      2. IV fluid bolus
    3. Is there a suspected pre-existing metabolic acidosis? (See Pearls)
      1. Sodium bicarbonate 50 mEq bolus IV/IO; may repeat x 2
      2. IV fluid bolus
    4. Is hypovolemia suspected? If yes, give fluid bolus.
    5. Is hypoxia suspected? If yes, administer high-flow oxygen and manage airway per Airway Algorithm, Blue 2.
    6. Do you suspect a pneumothorax? If yes, perform needle decompression.
    7. Is patient's core temperature less than 30°C (86°F) and circumstances consistent with hypothermia? If yes, see Severe Hypothermia WITHOUT signs of Life protocol, Yellow 12.

  2. Upon ROSC:

    1. Refer to Adult Post-Resuscitation Care protocol, Red 16.
    2. Contact OLMC for options of:
      1. Post-resuscitation amiodarone bolus (mix in D₅W)

Pearls for Cardiac Arrest:
Effective chest compressions and defibrillation are the most important therapies for the patient in cardiac arrest. Effective chest compressions are defined as:
a. A rate of 100 - 120 compressions/minute
b. Depth of between 2 - 2.5 inches
c. Allow for complete chest recoil
d. Minimize interruptions in compressions
e. Rotate rescuers every 2 minutes
f. Avoid respiratory rate greater than 10/min
g. Quantitative end-tidal CO₂ should be used to monitor effectiveness of chest compressions. If EtCO₂ is < 10 mmHg, attempt to improve chest compression quality
h. Consider additional monitoring with biometric feedback

Resuscitate on scene as the effectiveness of chest compressions decreases during any patient movement. Resuscitation on scene should be the goal, with the only exceptions being safety concerns for the responding crew OR inability to effectively resuscitate in the patient's current location.
